The House & Its Arms

The Nakashidze were a tavadi — a princely family — of Georgia, rooted in the south-western region of Guria and Adjara. Like other noble Georgian houses absorbed into the Russian Empire, their princely dignity was confirmed within the imperial nobility, and with it their coat of arms was entered into the great armorials of the age.

The best-known bearer of the name in Batumi was Prince Mikheil Nakashidze (1861–1935), entrepreneur, city councillor, and builder of the family house on Ninoshvili Street — the same house that lends this site its name. The arms below are his family's, reproduced in full on the commemorative plate created to connect their descendants and relatives across Georgia and North America.

No single "official" drawing of the arms exists. Instead the design survives in several documented renderings — a Petrograd manuscript armorial of 1922, a Moscow reference work of 1998, and a modern Georgian reconstruction of 2019 — each agreeing on the family's charges while differing in colour and arrangement. They are gathered here as a lasting reference.

Reading the Arms

The Blazon

The achievement below — a modern rendering — shows the elements common to the family's arms: a princely mantle and crown enclosing a shield quartered with its charges.

✠

The Mantle & Crown

An ermine-lined crimson mantle beneath a princely crown — the outward mark of tavadi rank, enclosing the whole shield.

I

Crossed Blades & Coronet

A golden coronet set above crossed blades — an emblem of martial honour and princely office, repeated in the opposing quarter.

II

Three Crescents

Upon azure, three silver crescents — a charge carried through every recorded version of the arms.

III

The Golden Bull

A golden bull upon a green mount, accompanied by a cross — a Caucasian emblem of strength and standing, and the most distinctive charge of the shield.

The Record

Documented Variants

Four renderings of the same arms, drawn a century apart. The sources agree on the family's charges; the colours and quartering shift from one hand to the next.

Manuscript · 1922

The Caucasian Armorial

A hand-drawn variant with a distinctive arrangement of pelicans and a spiral figure, preserved in a Petrograd manuscript on the princely houses of the Caucasus.

V. Tsikhinsky, Caucasian Armorial — Tavadi (Princely) Families (manuscript), Petrograd, 1922, p. 96.
Reference · 1998

Nobility of the Empire

The scholarly reconstruction published in the standard modern reference on the noble families of the Russian Empire, alongside its earlier engraved forms.

S. Dumin & P. Grebelsky, Noble Families of the Russian Empire, vol. IV, Likominvest, Moscow, 1998, p. 184.
Reconstruction · 2019

The Georgian Revival

A modern Georgian reconstruction of the family arms, restoring the design and its detail from the historical record.

Concept & detailing by Giorgi (Gaga) Mikelashvili; artwork by Giorgi Kontridze, 2019.

The Object in Your Hands

The Commemorative Plate

This page is the destination of the QR code printed on the reverse of the Nakashidze commemorative plate. The plate carries the arms in full colour on its face; its reverse names them in three tongues and points here, so that whoever holds it may read the history behind the heraldry.

The plates connect two family gatherings: relatives in Georgia received them in Tbilisi on 15 July 2026; relatives from Canada and the United States receive them in Toronto on 18 July 2026. One object carries the same Georgian family story across both places.
